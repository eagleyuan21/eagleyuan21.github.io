About Me

Welcome to my page! I am a current undergraduate Computer Engineering and Computer Science student at Northeastern University. My interests include building software and models, working with artificial intelligence and robotics, and learning more about high performance computing, space exploration, flight dynamics, mathematics, and physics. My biggest passion is learning new things and working with new technologies. Working on new projects (like this website) is a real joy of mine. Some of my hobbies include playing and watching sports, video games, violin, working with RC machines, superheroes, and movies. Some of my thoughts and opinions on my hobbies are posted on my blog page, which can be accessed through the navigation bar.

I first became inspired to pursue engineering when my dad took me to a science conference in San Francisco when I was 7. Ironically, it was actually the flight to and back that inspired me to pursue engineering, not the science conference. The plane fascinated me, so I started making paper airplanes and transitioned into building and flying RC planes and helicopters. My inspiration to pursue computer science originates from my hometown Oak Ridge, Tennessee, home to the Oak Ridge National Laboratory. Observing the world's fastest supercomputer Summit, understanding my dad's work, and conducting computer science research revealed to me the powers of computers, inspiring me to pursue computer science.

My dad got the idea to name me Eagle a few days after my birth when he was watching Tiger Woods play golf. Above the TV was a kite of an eagle that my aunt gave to my parents when they left China to the USA, and when I was born in Madison, Wisconsin, the apartment complex we lived in was called Eagle Heights. Realizing that someone named their child Tiger, he decided Eagle would be acceptable.

  3. Research Intern at Oak Ridge National Laboratory

    6/4/18 to 5/15/19

    I was fortunate enough to be selected to research at the Center for Nanophase Materials Science at the Oak Ridge National Laboratory. I worked on creating an Agent-Based Model that mimicked the foraging behavior of Black Soldier Fly Larvae. These larvae are of particular interest because of their ability to feed on organic waste, and in turn the larvae can be used as protein feeds. This process is environmentally friendly, so being able to understand eating behaviors can optimize the rate of recycling. Further data revealed that these larvae form a vortice in the 2d plane when eating, which is reminiscent of natural phenomena behaviors like flocking and swarming. Therefore, Netlogo was used to create the Agent-Based model. Once the model was finished, a genetic algorithm was implemented to minimize the fitness function and find the optimal parameter set. The main work for this project including models and reports can be found on my GitHub in the BSFL-ABM directory. This research won 1st place in the computer science division and 4th place overall at the Southern Appalachian Science and Engineering Fair (affiliated regional fair of ISEF).
